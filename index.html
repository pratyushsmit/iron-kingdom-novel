The Weight of the Sky

The weight of the sky felt heavier than the steel in Kaelen’s hand. For twelve hours, the Narrow Pass had been a meat grinder of mud and screaming iron. Kaelen, a veteran of more winters than he cared to count, stood at the lip of the ridge.

His breathing was a ragged whistle, his vision blurred by a cocktail of sweat and drying blood. He wasn’t a king, and he wasn’t a hero of legend—he was the wall.

Kaelen overlooks the devastation of the Narrow Pass as the armies clash below.

The enemy, the Iron Legion, had paused. They were regrouping at the base of the slope, their black banners snapping in the gale. To Kaelen’s left, a boy no older than seventeen fumbled with a broken shield.

"Steady, Pip. They put their boots on one foot at a time, just like you. And they die just as easy."

Geometry and Luck

"There are hundreds of them," Pip whispered. "And there is only one path," Kaelen replied, gesturing to the rocky bottleneck. "Geometry is a kinder mistress than luck. Here, their numbers mean nothing. Only their front rank matters."

As the horn sounded again—a low, mournful groan that vibrated in Kaelen’s chest—he adjusted his stance. Being a warrior wasn't about the swing; it was about the leverage. He shifted his center of gravity, calculating the slope's angle.

Steel meets steel in the brutal geometry of the front line.

The Legion charged. The first wave hit like a physical tide. Kaelen didn’t slash; he thrust. Short, punchy movements. He used his shield not just for defense, but as a weapon, slamming the rim into throats and knees.

"Every movement had to be perfect, because a single breath wasted was a second closer to the grave."

In the Shadow of Giants

An hour bled into two. Kaelen’s muscles screamed. His sword arm felt like it was made of lead, and a shallow cut above his eye sent a stinging trickle of red into his vision. Then came their captain—a giant of a man clad in fluted plate armor.

He swung a heavy mace that shattered the stone where Kaelen had stood a heartbeat before. Kaelen didn't retreat. He stepped in. In the dance of death, the closest space is often the safest. He bypassed the reach of the mace, stepping into the giant’s shadow.

The clash between the lone veteran and the armored titan of the Legion.

He used the man’s own momentum against him, a quick trip of the heel and a shove against the heavy breastplate. The giant tumbled backward, his weight sending him sliding down the muddy scree, taking three of his own men with him.

"In the dance of death, the closest space is often the safest."

The Aftermath

The Fear of a Warrior

When the sun finally dipped below the horizon, the Legion pulled back. The pass held. Kaelen sat on a blood-stained crate, his hands shaking too hard to unlace his helm. Pip sat beside him, miraculously alive.

"How do you do it?" Pip asked. "How are you not afraid?" Kaelen finally pulled his helm off, revealing graying hair and eyes that had seen too much. He looked at the boy and gave a tired, crooked smile.

Weary survivors catch their breath as the dusk settles over the battlefield.

"I’m terrified every second, Pip. But a warrior isn't someone who lacks fear. A warrior is just someone who’s decided that what they’re standing in front of is more important than what’s coming at them."

"Now, help me up. We have to sharpen the steel. Tomorrow is another day."
